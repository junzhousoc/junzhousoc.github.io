Female textile workers on shop floor

Female wanghong seller on platform

How gender plays out in economy across time and space?
Straddling huge fan bases and booming e-commerce, a new form of online entrepreneurship akin to social media influencers in the United States, but unique to China—wanghong (online celebrity) attracts millions of ordinary women, including those from lower-class backgrounds and lower-tier cities, to chase lucrative careers. According to CBNData, China’s wanghong economy was worth $9.2 billion in 2016. However, unlike Instagram influencers in the United States, the emerging wanghong industry in China has seamlessly connected digital economy and manufacturing by capitalizing on two forms of feminized labor—wanghong on platforms and dagongmei (female migrant workers) on shop floors—to transform the supply chain. Public media reported that “A top online influencer can generate sales of $20 million by modeling her own clothing brands in just the first 6 minutes of a live-streaming event, and if she stops the live-streaming, at least 40 factories will be immediately paralyzed.” At the same time, this online retailing relies heavily on the manufacturing process conducted by exploited dagongmei who only earn $15 a day on shop floors. The contrasting image of two groups of female workers tied to the same economic process raises a perplexing question: How does the shifting economy in China give rise to these two prominent models of female labor? read research
